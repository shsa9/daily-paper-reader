Abstract 

We develop a bonded-particle model for magneto-elastic rods that unifies large deformations, contact, and long-range magnetic interactions within a single discrete-element framework. The rod is discretized into orientable particles con-nected by co-rotational bonds that capture stretching, shearing, bending, and twisting through a symmetric decomposition of relative displacement and rota-tion. Magnetic coupling is introduced at the particle level: each particle carries a dipole moment that rotates with it, enabling both external-field actuation and long-range dipole–dipole interactions without modifying the structural formula-tion. We implement the model in LAMMPS to take advantage of its parallel efficiency, long-range electrostatic solvers, and multiphysics capabilities. We val-idate the framework against three benchmark problems: writhing instabilities of straight and curved rods under extreme twisting, large deflections of magnetized beams in uniform and constant-gradient fields, and mechanical hysteresis of heli-cal rods with dipole–dipole interactions. To demonstrate multiphysics capability, we couple the model with a lattice Boltzmann fluid solver via the immersed boundary method and simulate filaments in oscillatory channel flow and fluid pumping by magnetically actuated cilia arrays. Across all examples, the model shows good agreement with experimental, analytical, and numerical reference results.

# 1 Introduction 

Hard-magnetic soft materials have emerged as a promising platform for untethered soft robots, as they can be programmed and remotely controlled via external mag-netic fields [1, 2]. This has opened new possibilities in microrobotics for biomedical applications [3, 4]. Additionally, magnetic dipole–dipole interactions can be har-nessed to induce multistability [5, 6] and mechanical hysteresis [7–9], and form self-assembling network structures [10, 11], opening new avenues for metamaterial design [12]. Accurately and efficiently simulating such systems in complex environ-ments is challenging due to the interplay of large deformations, nonlocal interactions, contact, and other external forces. In numerous applications, the fundamental building blocks are beams or rods, owing to their capacity for large deformations and complex shape morphing. These have been used to design bio-inspired swimmers [3], robots [13], magneto-mechanical metamaterials [14], surgical catheters [15, 16], and artificial cilia [17, 18]. For hard-magnetic rods, reduced-order models based on Kirchhoff and Cosserat rod theories augmented with the Zeeman energy offer efficient alternatives to full three-dimensional simulations [8, 19–22]. These models have been extended to include dipole–dipole interactions [21, 23] and have been implemented in various simulation frameworks, including those capable of fluid–structure interaction [24, 25]. In geome-chanics, bonded-particle models are widely used to simulate elasticity and fracture in 2D and 3D solids [26–29]. Yet their application to 1D structures undergoing extreme deformation remains relatively unexplored, despite their natural suitability for soft matter systems across scales—from polymers to mechanical metamaterials. Although several studies have applied bonded-particle models to simulate rods and fibers [29– 31], they remain limited to pure planar bending or twisting cases and their stability remains unproven under scenarios of extreme deformation. Moreover, these methods remain underutilized despite their promise in simulating magneto-elastic materials. In this work, we develop a bonded-particle model for magneto-elastic rods that integrates seamlessly with discrete element and molecular dynamics frameworks, offer-ing a unified treatment of large deformations, contact, and long-range magnetic interactions. We implement our model in LAMMPS [32], leveraging its robust infras-tructure for large-scale systems, long-range electrostatics, and multiphysics coupling. The paper is organized as follows: Sec. 2 introduces the bonded-particle model for magneto-elastic rods, covering the elastic rod formulation, dynamics, contact, and magneto-elastic coupling; Sec. 3 validates the model against three benchmark prob-lems: straight and curved rods under extreme twisting; magnetized beams in uniform and constant-gradient fields; and helical rods with dipole–dipole interactions; Sec. 4 presents coupling with a lattice Boltzmann fluid solver, validated by simulating fila-ments in an oscillatory flow and fluid pumping by magnetic cilia arrays; and finally, Sec. 5 offers concluding remarks and an outlook on future directions. 22 Bonded-particle model for magneto-elastic rods 

While the term bonded-particle model originates from Potyondy and Cundall [26], we use it here broadly to refer to any non-contact bonded interaction for modeling solid behavior. The simplest models treat a solid as a network of simple springs [33]; these are sometimes called mass-spring models (MSM) or lattice-spring models (LSM). Lloyd et al. [34] optimized the MSM for triangular (2D) and tetrahedral (3D) meshes and obtained good agreement with FEM, though the Poisson’s ratio cannot be indepen-dently specified ( ν2D = 1 /3 [35] and ν3D = 1 /4 [36]). Furthermore, they found that, in 3D, a volume correction term is needed for better accuracy. Clemmer et al. [37] used an MSM based on random sphere packing and introduced a deviatoric term—computed from the difference between local volumetric dilation and bond stretching—to access a larger range of Poisson’s ratios. Zhang [38] derived a lattice-spring equivalent of a hexahedral mesh element for neo-Hookean solids, which also includes a volume cor-rection term. This was later extended by Ye et al. [39] to include magnetic actuation. However, adding dipole–dipole interactions is not straightforward since the nodes are point particles. More generally, purely radial springs are insufficient for capturing the coupling between shear and rotation [40]. Some bonded-particle models address these limitations by incorporating shear and rotational springs through finite-sized, orientable particles. This also enables dipoles to be assigned directly to the discrete elements. However, the added rotational degrees of freedom make it difficult to derive the correct spring parameters from macroscopic elasticity in 2D and 3D [41]. For 1D systems, many bond models based on rod theory exist [42, 43]. Chen et al. [29] reviewed various bonded-particle models and unified rod-based bond models into a generic form. We build upon the formulation of Wang et al. [40, 41, 44, 45], here-after referred to simply as the BPM. 1 In particular, Wang [44] developed a technique that decomposes the relative rotation into two rotations: one axial rotation around the bond and one rotation about an axis perpendicular to the bond. Moreover, the rotation angles are sequence-independent, which uniquely determines a twisting and a bending angle. This method only requires the initial (reference) configuration and the current configuration, making it more stable than incremental formulations using linearized rotation vectors [44]. In this paper, we use the terms rod and beam inter-changeably. Beams commonly refer to bending-only structures, as in Sec. 3.2, though 

beam elements in the FEM literature may include torsion as well.

## 2.2 Dynamics and contact 

The model described above was implemented as a custom bond style in the BPM package [37] in LAMMPS [32]. The equations of motion are integrated using velocity Verlet for translations and a Richardson update for quaternion orientations. The bond model also supports optional damping for each deformation mode. Radial damping is applied using the relative velocity projected along the bond axis, fr += gr (∆ v · ˆrc). For the angular deformation modes, strain rates are estimated by finite differences of the stored deformation measures: ˙ γ = ( γn − γn−1)/∆t for the shear angle, and sim-ilarly for the twist ˙ψ and bending ˙θ angles. Rate-proportional terms are then added to the corresponding elastic forces and torques, fs += gs rc ˙γ (with the associated τs

updated accordingly), τt += gt ˙ψ, and τb += gb ˙θ, where the g coefficients are the corresponding damping factors. The bond-level damping targets high-frequency oscil-lations between neighboring particles, while global Langevin damping is applied at the particle level to dissipate low-frequency kinetic energy and aid convergence toward quasi-static equilibrium. For dynamic simulations, the moment of inertia of each dis-crete element is that of a solid sphere, which differs from the cylindrical segment it 6represents. However, for quasi-static problems this discrepancy is inconsequential, as inertial terms vanish at equilibrium and the static configuration is determined entirely by the bond forces and torques, which are independent of particle shape. This limita-tion could be lifted by allowing user-defined diagonal inertia tensors, which would be a straightforward extension. Since the bonded-particle method originated in geomechanics, our model benefits from the readily available, well-established granular contact models. Our formula-tion is therefore applicable to elasto-granular coupling problems [54, 55], which we leave for future work. In the examples of Sec. 3.1 and 3.3, granular interactions are included only to prevent rod self-intersection; this is handled by a Hertz–Mindlin con-tact model between non-bonded particles. A limitation of this approach is that, to prevent particles from passing through the gaps between bonded neighbors, the par-ticle diameter must be at least as large as the bond length, and preferably larger to produce a smoother contact surface. This constraint couples the contact geometry to the discretization, removing the freedom to independently refine the BPM resolution. A bond-based contact model, in which contact is detected between cylindrical bond segments rather than spheres, would decouple these concerns. For example, Crassous [56] developed such an approach by combining a discrete elastic rod model with a Cundall–Strack contact model between cylindrical bonds.

# 3 Numerical validation 

In this section, we provide three numerical examples to validate the model. First, we study the extreme twisting of straight and curved rods to test purely elastic large-deformation behavior. Next, we consider magnetized beams in uniform and constant-gradient fields to demonstrate magnetic actuation. Finally, we examine helical rods with dipole–dipole interactions. 

## 3.1 Straight and curved rods under extreme twisting 

The writhing instabilities and plectoneme formation in twisted elastic rods have been extensively studied, both analytically and experimentally [65–68], and have served as benchmark problems for discrete rod models [51, 52, 69, 70]. Here, we apply our model to simulate the twisting of heavy elastic rods, initially compressed such that they buckle under their own weight, as studied by Lazarus et al. [71, 72] and fur-ther investigated by Cottanceau et al. [51], Grange and Bertrand [52]. Two cases are considered: a straight rod and a naturally curved rod.

## 3.3 Helical rods with dipole–dipole interactions 

Having validated magnetic actuation, we now turn to dipole–dipole interactions. Heli-cal rods with intra-rod magnetic attraction have been experimentally realized by Sano et al. [8], and later numerically studied by Sano [23] and Huang et al. [21]. Sano [23] added a dipole–dipole potential energy term to the Kirchhoff-like theory developed by Sano et al. [8]. Meanwhile, Huang et al. [21] developed a model based on discrete dif-ferential geometry (DDG). In both these approaches, the continuous rod is discretized into a set of nodes and edge vectors. Unlike in the BPM, the nodes do not rotate; instead, the edge vectors rotate relative to some material frame. Therefore, the dipoles are assigned to the edge vectors. In Sano et al. [8], the hMRE was cast to a helical unstrained configuration and mag-netized along the screw axis. If the dipole–dipole attraction is sufficiently strong, the helix will initially adopt a contracted configuration. Then applying a constant-gradient magnetic field along the screw axis produces an approximately constant body force on each discrete segment. By varying the strength of ∇B, the helix can be stretched or compressed along this axis. Perhaps the most interesting result is the ability to pro-gram hysteretic response during the loading–unloading cycle by tuning the strength of the dipole–dipole interactions. Hysteresis arises because the field gradient required to detach the helix exceeds the value at which it contracts. A helix of radius R and pitch ψ can be arc-length-parametrized by 

⟨R cos ( Ks ), R sin ( Ks ), s cos ψ⟩, where K = R−1 sin ψ. Here, K = √κ2 + τ 2, where 

κ = K sin ψ is the curvature and τ = K cos ψ is the torsion. We consider the example in Fig. 2 of Sano [23] with the following helix geometry: L = 103 mm, L/ (2 πR ) = 1 .64, and ˜ψ := ψ/ (π/ 2) = 0 .96. The helix was discretized into N = 103 bonds, making the bond length approximately l = 1 mm. The rod diameter is d = 2 mm. 13 4 Fluid–structure interaction 

Many applications of magneto-elastic rods involve operation in a fluid environment, where hydrodynamic forces fundamentally shape the system’s dynamics and vice versa—from fluid-pumping artificial cilia to bio-inspired swimming robots [3, 4, 15, 18]. Capturing this behavior requires coupling the solid mechanics model with a fluid solver. For particle-based solid models, the immersed boundary method (IBM) cou-pled with the lattice Boltzmann method (LBM) is an increasingly popular choice, particularly in biophysics and microfluidics [74]. For slender structures specifically, recent works include coupling a Cosserat rod model with an isogeometric IBM [75] and with a velocity–vorticity Navier–Stokes formulation [24]. IBM decouples the fluid grid from the structural representation, accommodating large deformations and com-plex geometries without remeshing. The LBM recovers macroscopic flow quantities from the evolution of particle distribution functions via collision and streaming steps on a fixed Cartesian grid. Owing to its locality and flexibility, LBM exhibits good par-allel scalability and is naturally suited for coupling with the domain-decomposition framework used in LAMMPS. Accordingly, several LBM implementations are available within LAMMPS [76–78].

# 5 Discussion and conclusion 

We have presented a bonded-particle model for magneto-elastic rods that treats each rod as a chain of orientable dipolar particles connected by co-rotational bonds. In the examples considered, our framework accurately reproduces the writhing instabilities of straight and curved rods under extreme twisting (Sec. 3.1), the large deflections of magnetized beams in non-uniform fields (Sec. 3.2), the mechanical hysteresis of helical rods with dipole–dipole interactions (Sec. 3.3), and the fluid pumping by magnetic cilia arrays (Sec. 4.3). We briefly discuss our choice of discretization, which largely depends on the sys-tem considered. For problems governed only by external magnetic loading, the BPM can be accurate with as few as ten bonds, as is the case with a cantilever beam under an end load [29–31]; we verify this with a convergence analysis in Supplemen-tary Information S3.1. Here, the discretization need only be fine enough to capture the local distribution of magnetic force and torque along the rod. For instance, the 19 magnetized beam of Sec. 3.2, Fig. 6(a), reduces to a cantilever beam problem under a slope-dependent moment and body force, and a convergence study in Supplementary Information S3.3 confirms that the tip deflection converges linearly with increasing number of bonds. This is lower than the quadratic or higher-order convergence typical of finite element and geometrically exact rod discretizations, and it represents the prin-cipal numerical trade-off of the BPM approach. The advantage, however, is that the particle-level description makes it straightforward to retrofit additional multiphysics interactions—magnetic, fluidic, granular, or otherwise—without modifying the struc-tural formulation. For the dipole–dipole interactions of Sec. 3.3, the discretization is no longer unconstrained: the point-dipole approximation becomes inaccurate when individual segments are very long or very flat. Similarly, for the contact treatment in Sec. 3.1, the particle radius sets the contact surface geometry; a bond-based con-tact model would be needed to decouple the elastic and contact discretizations. These constraints on bond, magnetic, and contact resolution can conflict, and in general the different physical interactions may require separate discretizations. All examples presented here employ dissipative dynamics: the validation cases of Sec. 3 use Langevin dynamics to reach quasi-static equilibria, and the cilia array of Sec. 4.3 is explicitly damped by a viscous fluid. Strict energy conservation was there-fore neither required nor enforced. Applying the BPM to conservative Hamiltonian dynamics would require addressing sources of energy drift. Most importantly, the energy in Eq. (10) is only an approximation, and the rotation decomposition does not straightforwardly yield a global energy from which forces and torques can be obtained as gradients with respect to particle positions and orientations; addressing this is a key direction for future work. Although the examples in this work are restricted to rod-like structures, the bonded-particle framework is not. The BPM was originally developed for three-dimensional bond networks in granular and cementitious materials, and the co-rotational beam bonds introduced here can connect particles in arbitrary topologies—such as those in architected metamaterials [87–89]. In the future, extend-ing the formulation to shell-like and solid structures will allow us to simulate more complex systems.

# S1 Notes on the LAMMPS implementation 

To run a LAMMPS simulation, we need an input script and (optional) data file. The input script is a domain-specific language and contains all the instructions to run the simulation from start to finish. Each line is a command , whose details can be found in the official documentation (https://docs.lammps.org/). Here, we use monospace font to denote LAMMPS commands/styles. Some functionalities require additional 

packages to be enabled before building LAMMPS. In this work, we use the following packages: BPM, DIPOLE, GRANULAR, LEPTON. 

## S1.1 BPM package 

The BPM package was developed by Clemmer et al. [1], providing the framework on which the present model is built. The atom style bpm/sphere defines spheri-cal particles with orientation represented by a unit quaternion. BPM particles are integrated in time using velocity-Verlet for translations and the Richardson update for quaternion orientations, as implemented in fix nve/bpm/sphere . The update dipole keyword/value pair can be added to also rotate the dipole moments with the quaternions. The elastic rod model developed in this paper is implemented as 

bond style bpm/corotational (release date TBD). A bond is defined using a bond ID, a bond type, and two atom IDs. This can be in the data file or by using 

create bonds .For the plectoneme example, pair style granular was used to model contact. To prevent particles from passing through the gap between two bonded particles, their diameter is set to 2–3 times the bond length. Then the pair interaction was disabled for particles connected up to three bonds away using the command special bonds lj 0 0 0 .

## S1.2 Magnetic dipoles 

The fix efield command is used to apply a uniform magnetic field, and the fix efield/lepton command is used for a non-uniform magnetic field. Magnetic dipole– dipole interaction can be added using pair style lj/cut/dipole/cut or other variants. For the rotation-based constitutive model used in this work [2], the update dipole keyword–value pair must be added to fix nve/bpm/sphere .With magnetic dipoles, it is best to use units lj or units cgs . Since the efield 

commands were made for electric dipoles, other unit systems may have internal conversion factors that users should be aware of. For example, in units si the dipole– dipole energy will have a prefactor (4 πε 0)−1 = 8 .9876 × 10 9 Nm 2/C2 (Coulomb constant), so the consistent magnetic units should be μ∗ = c [Cm] = 3 × 10 8 [Am 2]and B∗ = c−1 [V /m] = 3 .33 × 10 −9 [T]. In this work, we only considered permanent dipoles. But other magnetization models can also be used. For example, a simple superparamagnetic model can be implemented using the fix set command to update the dipoles directly together 2with pair style lj/cut/dipole/cut . More complicated magnetization models may require users to modify the LAMMPS source code.

Its temperature scale is set by {kB }, the numerical value of the Boltz-mann constant in the chosen LAMMPS unit system. In lj , {kB } = 1, while in cgs ,

## S2.1 Symmetric rotational decomposition 

In the BPM of Wang et al. [3–6] (and most beam-bond models [7]) one particle is chosen as the reference, from which the motion of the other particle is decomposed. However, we found that for non-planar deformations, Wang [5]’s decomposition algo-rithm for finite rotation is not permutation invariant; i.e., for a given bond, different forces and torques will be computed depending on which particle was chosen as the 3reference. This is because the non-commutativity of rotations makes it impossible to prescribe the same rotation order for both particles, leading to different bending planes and therefore different bending and rotational shear terms. This reference frame dependence is not unique to Wang [5]’s formulation; co-rotational models generally require specification of a distinguished frame, and many choices are possible [8, 9]. We modify the model by introducing a central frame obtained by averaging the orien-tation of the two particles using quaternion spherical linear interpolation, similar to the model of Obermayr et al. [9]. We measure the shear from this frame, which also combines into one translational shear term the otherwise separate translational and rotational shear components. For a planar beam with cross section ( w, h ) bending about the w axis, I = wh 3/12. When there is no twisting, the spring constants Kr, Ks, and Kb can be consistently defined even for non-cylindrical bonds.

# S3 Numerical validation for planar bending 

We performed bending tests to validate the BPM parameters. For a rectangular pris-matic beam of dimensions ( L, W, h ), where h ≪ L, we let W be along the third dimension and bending axis. Therefore, the cross-sectional area is A = W h and second moment of area is I = W h 3/12.

For the first test, we simulated cantilever beams ( L/h = 100, E = 1, ν = 0 .5) under a static point load or uniform load. ## S3.2 Cantilever beam under dynamic loading 

Next, we consider the example in Le et al. [16].
