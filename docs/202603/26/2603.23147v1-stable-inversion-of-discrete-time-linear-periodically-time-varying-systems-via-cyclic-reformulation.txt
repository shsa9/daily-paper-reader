# Stable Inversion of Discrete-Time Linear Periodically Time-Varying Systems via Cyclic Reformulation ∗

Hiroshi Okajima 

Faculty of Advanced Science and Technology, Kumamoto University, Japan 

Keywords: Inverse systems; Periodically time-varying systems; Cyclic re-formulation; Stable inversion; Multirate systems. 

Abstract 

Stable inverse systems for periodically time-varying plants are es-sential for feedforward control and iterative learning control of mul-tirate and periodic systems, yet existing approaches either require complex-valued Floquet factors and noncausal processing or operate on a block time scale via lifting. This paper proposes a systematic method for constructing stable inverse systems for discrete-time lin-ear periodically time-varying (LPTV) systems that avoids these lim-itations. The proposed approach proceeds in three steps: (i) cyclic reformulation transforms the LPTV system into an equivalent LTI rep-resentation; (ii) the inverse of the resulting LTI system is constructed using standard LTI inversion theory; and (iii) the periodically time-varying inverse matrices are recovered from the block structure of the cycled inverse through parameter extraction. For the fundamental case of relative degree zero, where the output depends directly on the cur-rent input, the inverse system is obtained as an explicit closed-form time-varying matrix expression. For systems with periodic relative de-gree r ≥ 1, the r-step-delayed inverse is similarly obtained in explicit closed form via the periodic Markov parameters. The stability of the resulting inverse system is characterized by the transmission zeros of the cycled plant, generalizing the minimum phase condition from the LTI case. Numerical examples for both relative degree zero and higher relative degree systems confirm the validity of the stability conditions and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework, includ-ing exact input reconstruction via causal real-valued inverse systems.

For linear time-invariant (LTI) systems, stable inversion theory is well developed: if all transmission zeros lie strictly in-side the unit disk (the minimum phase condition), a causal stable inverse exists and can be constructed explicitly in state-space form [3, 4]. When nonminimum phase zeros are present, a bounded noncausal inverse can be obtained via exponential dichotomy [11, 9], at the cost of requiring future output information (preview). Many practical systems, however, exhibit periodically time-varying dy-namics that cannot be captured by an LTI model. Linear periodically time-varying (LPTV) systems arise naturally in multirate sampled-data control [22], position-dependent and non-equidistantly sampled systems [8, 9], and high-precision positioning stages [10]. Because the system matrices vary periodically, the transfer function framework underlying LTI inversion the-ory is no longer available, making the construction of stable inverse systems considerably more involved. Existing work on LPTV inversion falls into three main categories; a com-prehensive comparison of inversion-based approaches for feedforward and ILC, including extensions to time-varying systems, is given in [12]. The first is the stable inversion approach of van Zundert and Oomen [8, 9], developed for discrete-time LPTV systems. This approach adapts the exponential dichotomy framework of Devasia and Paden [11], originally developed for continuous-time nonlinear systems, to the discrete-time periodic setting via a Floquet-type factorization of the monodromy matrix. The resulting in-verse operator is defined on the doubly-infinite time axis and is exact in the infinite-horizon sense. However, it requires complex-valued Floquet fac-tors and noncausal processing, and does not yield a closed-form state-space realization suitable for direct causal implementation. The second category consists of lifting-based approaches for discrete-time LPTV systems. Zhu et al. [13] apply the standard lifting technique to transform a periodic system into an equivalent LTI system, then construct the stable inverse of the lifted system by combining stable inversion with optimal state transitions. More recently, Zhu et al. [14] propose a double lifting approach that handles non-minimum phase multivariate LPTV systems by formulating the inversion as a finite-dimensional matrix equation and applying the Moore–Penrose pseu-doinverse, guaranteeing L2-bounded inputs for finite-time tracking. How-ever, since both approaches are based on lifting, the resulting inverse op-erates on the N -step block time scale and produces a finite-time input se-quence rather than a step-by-step periodic state-space realization in the orig-inal time domain. The third category consists of signal-processing-oriented methods for discrete-time LPTV filters [16, 15, 17], which focus on FIR/IIR filter inversion using polyphase or modulator representations, or on sta-ble/unstable decompositions of LPTV digital filters. These methods are not formulated in a state-space control framework and typically assume the 2direct feedthrough term is nonzero (relative degree zero). Invertibility of linear time-varying discrete-time systems was studied in a foundational paper by Kono [7], which provides existence conditions for left and right inverses of general LTV systems in terms of rank conditions on structured matrices. This provides the theoretical underpinning for the inversion problem but does not give a constructive algorithm specialized to the LPTV case. A closely related line of work concerns the model matching problem for periodic systems. Colaneri and Kuˇ cera [18] showed that, via cyclic reformu-lation, a periodic state-feedback law can be designed so that the closed-loop input–output map matches a prescribed target transfer function. The zero-matching conditions in their framework are expressed through the cyclic transfer function and are thus closely related to the periodic transmission zero conditions that govern the stability of the inverse system in the present work. Tange and Tsumura [19] extended this perspective by formulating periodically weighted model matching problems using dual lifted forms for causal LPTV controllers, and showed that LPTV controllers can outper-form LTI ones in rejecting periodic disturbances. The inversion problem studied here can be viewed as a special case of model matching in which the target is the identity map; the key difference is that the present work derives an explicit periodic state-space realization of the inverse rather than a state-feedback gain. The present paper proposes a fundamentally different approach based on cyclic reformulation [20, 21]. Cyclic reformulation transforms an N -periodic LPTV system into an equivalent LTI system by a state, input, and output coordinate transformation that exploits the periodic structure. The key advantage over lifting [22] is that cyclic reformulation operates at the original sampling rate (rather than N -step block time) and preserves the step-by-step input-output relationship, while still enabling the direct application of LTI tools. This capability has been exploited to extend LTI design methods to the periodic setting without conservatism, including LMI-based controller design for multirate systems [23], multirate Kalman filter design with semidefinite noise covariances [24], and system identification of periodic plants [25]. The proposed inversion procedure consists of three steps: (i) cyclic reformulation transforms the LPTV plant into a cycled LTI system ˇP ;(ii) the inverse ˇP −1 is constructed using standard LTI inversion theory; and (iii) the periodically time-varying inverse matrices are extracted from the block structure of ˇP −1 through parameter extraction. This procedure yields explicit closed-form inverse systems for both the relative degree zero case (nonsingular Dk) and the higher relative degree case ( Dk = 0, periodic relative degree r ≥ 1), with the stability of the inverse characterized by the transmission zeros of the cycled plant. The contributions of this paper are as follows: 31. An inversion framework based on cyclic reformulation is proposed that reduces the LPTV inversion problem exactly to an LTI inversion prob-lem, avoiding complex-valued arithmetic and noncausal processing. 2. Explicit closed-form periodic inverse systems are derived for both rela-tive degree zero (Theorem 1) and periodic relative degree r ≥ 1 (Theo-rem 3), yielding causal state-space realizations at the original sampling rate. 3. The stability of the inverse system is characterized by the transmis-sion zeros of the cycled LTI plant, linking the periodic minimum phase condition to the Schur stability of the inverse monodromy matrix (The-orem 2 and Corollary 2). This paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents mathematical preliminaries on cyclic reformulation and reviews the relevant LTI inverse system theory. Section 3 presents the main results: the three-step inversion framework for relative degree zero systems, stability analysis, and extensions to higher relative degree. Section 4 demonstrates the approach through numerical examples. Section 5 discusses the relationship to existing methods and limitations, and Section 6 concludes the paper.

Remark 1 Cyclic reformulation differs from the standard lifting technique [22] in the following sense. Both the cycled and lifted representations have expanded state dimension N n . # 5 Discussion 

The proposed framework based on cyclic reformulation provides a unifying perspective on LPTV inversion that complements existing approaches. 

Comparison with van Zundert & Oomen [9] . The stable inversion ap-proach of [9] employs exponential dichotomy via Floquet theory to construct a bounded noncausal inverse on the doubly-infinite time axis. This approach 19 handles nonminimum phase LPTV systems at the cost of noncausality and requires complex-valued Floquet factors (Ψ 1/N in the notation of [9]). In contrast, the proposed method constructs a causal (or r-step-delayed) in-verse in the minimum phase case, uses only real arithmetic (all matrices Γk, Λ k, etc., are real-valued), and produces a finite-dimensional state-space realization immediately suitable for implementation. For minimum phase LPTV systems, the proposed approach is therefore preferable in practical applications. 

Comparison with lifting-based approaches [22, 13, 14] . Lifting the LPTV system over one period yields an equivalent LTI system of expanded state dimension N n , and an inverse of this lifted system can be constructed via standard LTI theory. Zhu et al. [13] combine lifting-based stable inversion with optimal state transitions for periodic systems. The more recent work [14] extends this framework to nonminimum phase multivariate LPTV sys-tems: a Smith–McMillan-based decomposition separates the system into maximum phase and minimum phase subsystems, and the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse of the lifted maximum phase part yields an L2-bounded input sequence that achieves exact tracking after an initial preparation window of 

T n o steps (where no is the number of nonminimum phase zeros). The re-sulting inversion is neither unstable nor approximate; it is a bounded, exact finite-time feedforward signal computed offline. The proposed method and these lifting-based approaches therefore differ in their scope of applicability and implementation characteristics. The lifting-based methods can handle nonminimum phase LPTV systems at the cost of sacrificing the initial tran-sient steps and requiring offline batch computation over a finite horizon. In contrast, the proposed cyclic reformulation approach is restricted to the minimum phase case but produces a periodic state-space system that op-erates at the original sampling rate and can be implemented as an online causal filter running indefinitely. The parameter extraction step (Step 3) is the key enabling step that recovers the time-phase-indexed matrices from the block structure of the cycled inverse. 

Relationship to Kono [7] . Kono’s invertibility conditions for LTV discrete-time systems provide necessary and sufficient conditions for the existence of left/right inverses in terms of rank conditions on time-indexed observabil-ity/controllability matrices. Theorems 1 and 3 and Corollary 1 provide, for the periodic case, explicit constructive formulas that are equivalent to the zero conditions of the cycled LTI plant, which are more directly computable than the time-indexed rank conditions of [7]. 

Connection to periodic model matching [18, 19] . The model matching problem solved in [18] seeks a periodic state-feedback law that assigns a target input–output map, subject to zero-matching conditions expressed in terms of the cyclic transfer function. The present inversion problem can be interpreted as a special instance of model matching in which the target is the identity transfer function; however, the solution form is fundamentally 20 different, as the present method produces a stand-alone periodic state-space system (observer-type realization) rather than a state-feedback gain acting on the plant. Nevertheless, the role of the periodic transmission zeros is common to both frameworks: in [18] the zeros constrain the achievable target models, while in the present work they determine the stability of the inverse system (Theorem 2). 

Role of the block-circulant structure and shift absorption . The key struc-tural insight underlying the parameter extraction step is that the cycled inverse system can always be brought into the standard cycled LTI form by absorbing the shift matrix ˇS rp into the input signal. For the cycled inverse ˇP −1 obtained from the LTI inversion of ˇP , the matrices ˇBinv and ˇDinv do not directly possess the cycled structure when r ≥ 1. However, the factoriza-tion ˇC ˇAr−1 ˇB = ˇS rp ˇM (Lemma 4) shows that the shift is entirely due to the phase mismatch between y(k + r) and the current time index k. Introducing ˇyr(k) = ˇS−rp ˇy(k + r) absorbs this mismatch, yielding ˇDinv ˇS rp = ˇM −1 (block-diagonal) and ˇBinv ˇS rp = ˇB ˇM −1 (1-shift block-circulant). This restores the full cycled LTI structure and enables the same block-by-block parameter extraction as in the relative degree zero case. 

Application to feedforward control and ILC . The proposed inverse sys-tem P −1 can be used directly as a feedforward controller or as the inversion model in iterative learning control [6, 12]. In the ILC context, I − P −1P

is identically zero when the exact inverse is used with matching initial con-ditions ( ζ(0) = x(0)). When initial conditions do not match, the tracking error decays geometrically, governed by the monodromy matrix Φ inv .

Extension to nonminimum phase systems . The present framework is restricted to the minimum phase case, where a causal stable inverse exists. When the cycled plant ˇP has transmission zeros outside the unit disk, the cycled inverse ˇP −1 is unstable, and a direct causal realization is not feasible. A possible approach is to apply the stable/unstable spectral decomposition to the cycled LTI system ˇP and construct a bounded noncausal inverse via the standard dichotomy approach [11], then recover the periodic inverse matrices through parameter extraction. 

# 6 Conclusion 

This paper has presented a systematic method for constructing stable inverse systems for discrete-time LPTV systems via cyclic reformulation. The pro-posed three-step procedure—cyclic reformulation, LTI inverse construction, and parameter extraction—reduces the LPTV inversion problem exactly to the well-studied LTI case; the reduction itself introduces no conservatism or approximation, though the resulting causal inverse is restricted to the periodically minimum phase case. For relative degree zero systems (Assumption 1), the inverse is given by 21 the explicit formulas (21)–(24), and stability is equivalent to the cycled plant being minimum phase, or equivalently, to the periodic transmission zeros ly-ing strictly inside the unit disk (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1). This stability characterization links the transmission zeros of the cycled LTI plant to the Schur stability of the inverse monodromy matrix Φ inv , providing a directly verifiable condition. For systems with periodic relative degree r ≥ 1 (As-sumption 2), the r-step-delayed inverse is obtained in explicit closed form via the periodic Markov parameters M (r) 

> k

(Theorem 3). The block-circulant structure of the cycled system is shown to be preserved under inversion, which is the key algebraic property enabling the parameter extraction. Since the resulting inverse system is a finite-dimensional periodic state-space sys-tem using only real-valued matrices, it can be implemented as an online filter at the original sampling rate (causal for r = 0, requiring r-step output preview for r ≥ 1), making it directly applicable to feedforward control and iterative learning control of multirate and periodic systems. Future work includes extending the framework to nonminimum phase LPTV systems via noncausal inversion of the cycled plant, treating systems with non-uniform relative degree across time phases, integrating the inverse system with feedback controller design for periodic systems [21], and ap-plying the method to multirate sampled-data systems where Assumption 2 may fail due to the structural zero pattern of multirate I/O matrices.
