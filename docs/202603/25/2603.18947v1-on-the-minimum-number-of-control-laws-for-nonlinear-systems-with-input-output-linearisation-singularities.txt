# ABSTRACT 

This paper addresses the fundamental question of determining the minimum number of distinct control laws required for global controllability of nonlinear systems that exhibit singularities in their feedback linearising controllers. We introduce and rigorously prove the (k+1) -Controller Lemma, which establishes that for an nth order single-input single-output nonlinear system with a singularity manifold parameterised by k algebraically independent conditions, exactly k+1 distinct control laws are necessary and sufficient for complete state-space coverage. The sufficiency proof is constructive, employing the approximate linearisation methodology together with transversality arguments from differential topology. The necessity proof proceeds by contradiction, using the Implicit Function Theorem, a dimension-counting argument and structural constraints inherent to the approximate linearisation framework. The result is validated through exhaustive analysis of the ball-and-beam system, a fourth-order mechanical system that exhibits a two-parameter singularity at the third output derivative. 

Keywords: approximate linearisation, ball-and-beam system, differential geometry, feedback linearisation, La-grangian mechanics, nonlinear control, relative degree, singularities, switched control systems, transversality theory 

# 1 Introduction 

Feedback linearisation stands as one of the most powerful and elegant techniques in nonlinear control theory, enabling the transformation of complex nonlinear systems into equivalent linear systems through judicious coordinate changes and state feedback [Isidori, 1995, Khalil, 2002, Nijmeijer and van der Schaft, 1990]. The central idea, developed exten-sively by Isidori, Krener and their collaborators during the 1980s, exploits the differential geometric structure inherent in nonlinear systems to cancel nonlinearities exactly, thereby yielding a linear input–output relationship amenable to the full arsenal of classical linear design techniques [Isidori et al., 1981, Jakubczyk and Respondek, 1980, Hunt et al., 1983]. However, the conditions needed for exact input–output linearisation are highly restrictive and frequently fail to hold globally across the entire state space. The seminal work of Jakubczyk and Respondek [1980] established necessary and sufficient conditions for exact state linearisation through the involutivity of certain distributions constructed from the system’s vector fields. When these geometric conditions fail, alternative approaches become necessary. Krener [1984] showed that approximate linearisation may be achieved even when exact conditions fail, while Byrnes and Isidori [1988] characterised the local stabilisation properties of minimum-phase nonlinear systems exhibiting such failures. A particularly important and challenging class of problems arises when the relative degree of a system becomes un-defined on certain submanifolds of the state space, creating singularities in the feedback linearising control law. 1.1 Literature review and motivation 

The geometric approach to nonlinear control has its intellectual roots in the pioneering work of Brockett [1978], who first considered certain forms of feedback in addition to pure state transformations. Hunt et al. [1983] provided fundamental results on global transformations of nonlinear systems, while the comprehensive theory was unified and extended by Isidori [1995] in his seminal treatise, which remains the standard reference in the field. The textbooks by Nijmeijer and van der Schaft [1990], Khalil [2002], Sastry [1999], Slotine and Li [1991] and Marino and Tomei [1995] provide accessible introductions to these geometric methods and their applications. The problem of systems with singularities has been addressed from various perspectives. Tomlin and Sastry [1998] considered switching through singularities, establishing conditions under which controllers could be switched while maintaining closed-loop stability. Liberzon [2003] developed a comprehensive theory of switching in systems and control, providing fundamental stability criteria for switched systems based on common Lyapunov functions and dwell-time conditions. The concept of patchy vector fields introduced by Ancona and Bressan [1999] guarantees the existence of finite controller families for asymptotic stabilisation of nonlinear systems, though crucially without quan-tifying the minimum number required. Sontag’s influential work on input-to-state stability [Sontag, 1989, Sontag and Wang, 1995] provides additional analytical tools for assessing the robustness of such switched control architectures. The ball-and-beam system provides an ideal canonical case study for investigating feedback linearisation singularities. This classical benchmark, found in control laboratories worldwide, exhibits all the essential features of systems with ill-defined relative degree while remaining sufficiently simple for complete analytical treatment. The foundational work of Hauser et al. [1992] demonstrated rigorously that the ball-and-beam fails the involutivity conditions for full state linearisation, yet showed that approximate input–output linearisation can nonetheless be achieved through careful modification of the system’s vector fields. Their work established the theoretical foundation for the approximate linearisation techniques that we extend and generalise here. The mathematical foundations underlying our proof rely on results from differential topology, particularly transversal-ity theory as developed in the classic texts by Hirsch [1976] and Guillemin and Pollack [1974]. The Implicit Function Theorem, which plays a central role in our arguments regarding the local structure of singularity manifolds, is treated in Krantz and Parks [2002]. We also draw upon the theory of smooth manifolds as presented in Lee [2012]. Despite this extensive body of literature, a fundamental theoretical question has remained unanswered: given a non-linear system with feedback linearisation singularities characterised by a specific geometric structure, what is the minimum number of distinct control laws required for global controllability? While practical designs using multiple controllers have appeared [Hauser et al., 1992, Tomlin and Sastry, 1998], and while patchy feedback theory [Ancona and Bressan, 1999] guarantees existence of finite controller families, no previous result has provided a sharp lower bound quantifying this minimum based on the intrinsic geometry of the singularity manifold.

2.5 Elements of differential topology 

Our proof relies on several concepts from differential topology [Hirsch, 1976, Guillemin and Pollack, 1974, Lee, 2012]. 

Definition 6 (Transversality) . Smooth submanifolds M, N ⊂ Rn are transverse at p ∈ M ∩ N , denoted M ⋔p N , if 

TpM + TpN = Rn. They are transverse ( M ⋔ N ) if this holds at every point of M ∩ N .

Lemma 1 (Codimension of Transverse Intersections [Hirsch, 1976]) . If M ⋔ N in Rn, then M ∩ N is a smooth submanifold with codim( M ∩ N ) = codim( M ) + codim( N ). In particular, if codim( M ) + codim( N ) > n , then generically M ∩ N = ∅.

Theorem 2 (Implicit Function Theorem [Krantz and Parks, 2002]) . Let F : Rn → Rm be smooth with m ≤ n. If 

F (x0) = 0 and dF (x0) has full row rank, then F −1(0) is a smooth submanifold of dimension n − m near x0.

9.1 Geometric interpretation 

The (k+1) -Controller Lemma has a transparent geometric reading: the factored coefficient a(x) = c(x) Qki=1 φi(x)

creates k independent hypersurface “obstacles” to global linearisability. Each factor produces a codimension-1 sin-gularity component requiring a dedicated approximate controller. The standard controller covers the regular region, totalling k + 1 .

9.3 Scope and limitations 

The necessity result applies specifically to controllers within the approximate linearisation framework, relying on the structural constraint that modifying f or g to eliminate one singularity factor preserves others (Theorem 7). Alternative paradigms (sliding mode, MPC, backstepping) might achieve coverage with fewer controllers. The sufficiency result is more general: k + 1 controllers always suffice provided the transversality conditions of Theorem 5 hold. Additional limitations include restriction to SISO systems, smooth nonlinearities, control-affine structure and m < n. Extensions to MIMO systems with decoupling matrix singularities, non-smooth systems and input-constrained systems remain open. 

# 10 Conclusions 

This paper established a fundamental result characterising the minimum control complexity for nonlinear systems with feedback linearisation singularities. The (k+1) -Controller Lemma proves that for an nth order SISO system with a k-parameter singularity manifold, exactly k + 1 distinct control laws are necessary and sufficient within the approximate linearisation framework. Sufficiency is constructive, using transversality to show that k approximate controllers plus one standard controller achieve full coverage.
