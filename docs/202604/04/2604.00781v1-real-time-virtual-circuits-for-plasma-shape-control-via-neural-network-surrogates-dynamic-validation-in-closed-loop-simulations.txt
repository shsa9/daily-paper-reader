Abstract —Reliable confinement and stable performance of tokamak fusion plasmas require accurate real-time magnetic shape control. A promising route to reduced latency and in-creased flexibility in plasma control systems (PCS) is to emulate physics-based controllers using neural networks. In prior work, we have demonstrated that virtual circuits (VCs), which define the poloidal field coil current vectors able to modify each plasma shape parameter independently, can be accurately emulated with neural network models trained on a large library of simulated Grad-Shafranov equilibria. This enables magnetic controllers to accurately adapt to evolving plasma equilibria, in contrast to pre-set VC schedules whose performance degrades upon departure from their reference equilibria. Here, we investigate the performance and robustness of these emulators in closed-loop simulations using the FreeGSNKE Pulse Design Tool (FPDT): a framework that couples the FreeGSNKE evolutive equilibrium solver with a virtual PCS. The FPDT models the coupling between controllers, plasma current and shape response, and ac-tuator constraints. Using the emulated VCs within the FPDT, we demonstrate effective in-silico control of MAST Upgrade (MAST-U) plasma scenarios and show that the emulators are robust in the presence of input measurement uncertainty and under different update frequencies. These results establish the viability of neural network emulated VCs for closed-loop plasma shape control, representing a key step toward real-time deployment in the MAST-U PCS. 

Index Terms —Virtual circuits, neural network emulation, plasma control, pulse design tool, FreeGSNKE, MAST-U 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Accurate real-time magnetic control of tokamak fusion plasma position and shape is essential for maintaining reliable confinement and enabling access to high-performance oper-ating regimes and advanced divertor configurations [1, 2]. To this end, the plasma control system (PCS) regulates deviations in geometrical shape parameters (e.g. midplane radii, X-point locations, strikepoints—typically under the assumption of toroidal symmetry) from prescribed reference waveforms by computing the required adjustments to poloidal field (PF) coil currents. These adjustments are enacted in real-time via PF coil voltage actuation, with sub-millisecond latency [3, 4]. Here, our focus is on calculating the adjustments in PF coil currents using virtual circuits (VCs) [5, 6]. A VC matrix decouples the shape control problem around a specific plasma equilibrium, i.e. it determines the PF coil current changes required to move each shape parameter independently, by one physical unit. Building a VC matrix is not currently feasible in real time as it requires a linearisation of the Grad-Shafranov (GS) equilibrium [7, 8] to obtain the sensitivities of the shape parameters. In classical plasma scenario design, VCs for a small number of target GS equilibria are calculated offline, to be used throughout a plasma discharge in a pre-set, phase-segmented manner. As their validity is only local, the accuracy of a VC matrix rapidly degrades as the plasma equilibrium evolves away from the reference state. As a result, the pre-calculated VCs may lose their effectiveness in producing orthogonal plasma responses, causing coil current adjustments to interfere with one another and potentially drive unnecessary stress on the PF coil power supplies. Prior work has demonstrated VCs can be accurately emu-lated using feedforward neural networks (NNs), based on real time tokamak diagnostic measurements [9, 10]. With inference times on the order of milliseconds, NN emulators enable state-aware decoupling of plasma shape control by providing VCs that are continuously updated with the evolving plasma

state. This can improve shape controller responsiveness and accuracy, particularly for complex shape displacements that would otherwise require segmented control strategies with a large number of pre-set time-phases and pre-computed VCs at the corresponding equilibrium waypoints. This contribution complements an increasing body of work related to plasma control using NNs [11, 12, 13, 14] and reinforcement learning [15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20]. In this paper, we validate the performance and test the robustness of emulated VCs for real-time shape control by deploying the NN models presented by [10] within closed-loop plasma equilibrium simulations using the open-source FreeGSNKE Pulse Design Tool (FPDT) [21, 22]. Previously validated against MAST Upgrade (MAST-U) discharges, the coupled evolutive equilibrium solver and virtual PCS within the FPDT provide an ideal test bed for assessing the emulated VCs in silico, in preparation for deployment. This ‘dynamic validation’ complements previous work on purely ‘static val-idation’ (more details in Section II), by accounting for the evolving currents in passive conducting structures and the interaction with other controllers in the PCS, both of which are neglected in static tests. It also enables monitoring of the plasma and coil responses to frequently changing VCs over time, providing a more realistic assessment of their impact on closed-loop plasma control. In Section II, we briefly summarise the process by which the emulated VCs are generated and how they were statically validated. We then explain, in Section III, how the emulated VCs are seamlessly integrated into the virtual PCS within the FPDT. In Section IV, we perform robustness tests and verify that emulated VCs can successfully control MAST-U plasma discharges in silico. II. E MULATED VIRTUAL CIRCUITS

B. Neural network emulation and static validation 

Here, we provide a brief description of the NN emulators, including the GS library generation, NN training, and static validation, but we refer the reader to [10] for further details. We use feedforward NNs to learn a mapping from 

(Iact , I p, θ) ∈ R17 to P ∈ R7 (where Iact ∈ R12 ), which we differentiate to derive sensitivity matrices and correspond-ing VCs. Firstly, Ipas itself has limited effect on S, and second, we expect real time deployment of the emulators to take place before being able to accurately estimate Ipas in real time on MAST-U [6]. In order to build the mapping above we used a library of synthetic plasma equilibria. Starting from experimentally observed “seed” equilibria from previous MAST-U campaigns, we used the random walk Markov Chain Monte Carlo method presented in [23] to explore the input space and build the training dataset. This resulted in a dataset comprised of approximately 900 k FreeGSNKE GS solutions spanning the operational space of MAST-U, including both experimentally observed and yet-to-be observed plasma configurations. The trained models typically contain 4-5 hidden layers and 128 -

256 nodes per layer, for a total of 1–5 × 10 5 trainable weights. We retain the top eight performing models, which we use to produce ensemble estimates of the Jacobians S using finite differences. Static validation of the emulators was carried out in [10] to assess the orthogonality of the plasma response, i.e. verifying that a requested change in one shape parameter would not change the others. This involved systematically comparing a requested 5 mm shape parameter change with the observed change obtained when applying either the emulated VC or a finite-difference-based VC from FreeGSNKE. III. I NTEGRATION WITH THE FREE GSNKE P ULSE DESIGN 

TOOL 

The FPDT is a simulation framework that enables a closed-loop interaction between the FreeGSNKE evolutive plasma simulator and a virtual PCS. Inspired by the MAST-U PCS, the virtual PCS used by the FPDT regulates plasma current, shape parameters, and vertical plasma position, simultaneously enforcing safety limits on coil currents and voltages 2. At each time step, the PCS takes in equilibrium-based measurements of plasma current, shape parameters, and vertical position, returning voltage commands to be applied to the active PF coils. Together with pre-prescribed plasma profiles θ and a plasma resistivity value, these voltages are passed to the simulator and used to advance the plasma and metal currents forward in time. Focusing on shape control at simulation time t, the Shape Controller provides a requested rate of change in P , computed from the measured shape parameters using a proportional-integral-derivative (PID) controller. These requests are used by the Virtual Circuits Controller to calculate the requested rate of change of the active PF currents:

B. Robustness against plasma profiles uncertainty 

In the absence of real-time inference for the plasma profiles 

θ, the emulated VCs will need to be provided with time dependent profile prescriptions derived from a reference shot with a similar ramp up, plasma heating profile, and fuelling strategy. V. D ISCUSSION 

In this paper, we have presented an in silico validation of emulated VCs for tokamak plasma shape control. We demonstrate that the emulated VCs can be successfully em-bedded within a (virtual) PCS framework, where they interact seamlessly with conventional, non-AI-based controllers. Their performance is maintained across a range of different up-dates times, confirming that even under conservative real-time constraints, where inference could take a few milliseconds in an operational PCS, they continue to perform effectively. In addition, the controllers remain robust under variations in plasma profile parameters, reflecting real discharge conditions in which the plasma may evolve away from the reference discharge. Similar performance is attained when the simulated measurement data (e.g. plasma current, coil currents, shape parameters) contains expected levels of measurement noise. The emulated VCs also retain strong performance in the presence of evolving passive structure currents, despite these currents not being included in the training process. Overall, these results show that emulated VCs are suit-able for real-time magnetic shape control. They are ex-pected to reduce reliance on intricate combinations of feedfor-ward/feedback scenario design, minimising the need for expert offline VC computations, thereby enabling greater automation within plasma control. This validation marks a step towards experimental verification and the eventual deployment of em-ulated VCs into an operational PCS. ACKNOWLEDGMENT
