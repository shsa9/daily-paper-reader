Abstract 

Slender magnetic elements provide a versatile platform for programmable shape-morphing under remote magnetic actuation. However, a general and physically interpretable framework for the inverse design of a ‘magneto-elastica’ under prescribed boundary conditions remains lacking. In this work, we develop an explicit analytical formulation for the inverse design of a magneto-elastica based on the integral form of the moment equilibrium equations. This approach yields direct constraints on the admissible curvature and rotation fields, enabling a systematic characterization of the feasible design space. We identify the key dimensionless parameters that govern the competition between magnetic torques and elastic restoring moments and show that the applied boundary conditions are an essential ingredient. We obtain closed-form solutions for the beam tapering profiles required to generate desired actuated shapes in the cases of clamped–free and clamped–clamped configurations; in the latter case, this includes analytical expressions for the boundary reactions. The formulation recovers the classical inverse elastica in the absence of magnetic fields and reveals a linear scaling between curvature deviation and magnetic mismatch. A tessellation strategy based on stiffness tailoring is further proposed for the design of discretized morphing surfaces. The theoretical predictions are validated against discrete elastic rod simulations and experiments across representative geometries. This work establishes a consistent analytical framework for the inverse design of a magneto-elastica and provides new insight into magnetically-induced shape programming in slender structures. 

Keywords: Magneto-elastica, Shape-morphing, Inverse design, Analytical framework

2010; Oliver et al., 2016; Sydney Gladman et al., 2016; Van Rees et al., 2017; Dudek et al., 2025). Such capabilities are central to applications including compact-to-deployed transformations (Gat-tas and You, 2015), reconfigurable locomotion and manipulation (Sun et al., 2023), and conformal interfacing with complex and time-varying surfaces (Qamar et al., 2018). Common strategies to achieve programmable morphing include geometric programming through architected layouts, ma-terial programming through built-in eigenstrain or intrinsic curvature, and field-driven actuation using remotely applied stimuli (Yang et al., 2023). In many cases, complex three-dimensional (3D) shapes are generated by designing low-dimensional building blocks, such as beams, ribbons, and plates, whose kinematics allow large deformations at relatively low energetic cost (Liu et al., 2020; Bo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2026). Among field-driven actuation mechanisms, magnetic actuation has emerged rapidly in recent years due to its remote and tunable nature (Bastola and Hossain, 2021). By embedding magnetic particles within elastic matrices, structures can undergo large and reversible deformations under externally applied magnetic fields (Kim and Zhao, 2022). Their mechanical response is governed by the competition between elastic bending and magnetically induced body torques, leading to rich nonlinear behaviors and a broad range of accessible configurations (Zhao et al., 2019). Magnet-ically actuated slender structures have therefore attracted significant attention as a platform for programmable shape-morphing (Lum et al., 2016; Hu et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2021). In particular, one-dimensional (1D) magnetic structures, such as beams and rods, are especially attractive thanks to their ability to undergo large, controllable deformations (Yan et al., 2022; Sano et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023). Such characteristics make them fundamental building blocks for morphing systems and have enabled applications in areas such as continuum robotics and minimally invasive devices, where flexible magnetic elements are used for navigation and manipulation (Kim et al., 2019; Kim and Zhao, 2022; Dreyfus et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2026). Inverse design plays a central role in exploiting these capabilities: the goal is to determine the actuation or structural parameters that will produce prescribed shapes. For slender structures, inverse design has been studied extensively in the context of the (non-magnetic) elastica, including strain-programmed bilayer ribbons (Chen et al., 2011; Armon et al., 2011; Li et al., 2025; Levin et al., 2021), buckling-driven rods with tailored intrinsic curvature and boundary conditions (Derouet-Jourdan et al., 2010, 2013; Bertails-Descoubes et al., 2018; Qin et al., 2022; Li et al., 2026), and stiffness-programmed structures (Liu et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2020; Hafner and Bickel, 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Hafner and Bickel, 2023). For the stiffness-programmed structures, a particularly convenient way of varying the stiffness spatially is to taper the strip width. However, most existing formulations are based on the differential form of the elastica equations, with inverse design achieved through local relations between curvature, loading, and material properties. While these approaches provide valuable theoretical insights, they do not explicitly reveal the global constraints imposed by boundary conditions and actuation, and therefore do not enable a systematic characterization of which shapes are attainable. The inverse design of the magneto-elastica introduces additional challenges beyond those en-countered in classical elastica (Lum et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2021, 2023; Li et al., 2025). Magnetic actuation enters the equilibrium equations as a distributed torque density, giving rise to intrinsi-cally nonlocal contributions to the internal moment (Wang et al., 2020). At the same time, the governing equations remain strongly nonlinear because of the geometric coupling between curva-ture and rotation (Sano et al., 2022). As a result, existing inverse design approaches based on the differential form of the elastica equations (Liu et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2020) do not directly reveal 2global constraints on admissible curvature and rotation fields, making it difficult to characterize the feasible design space under prescribed boundary conditions. In this work, we develop an explicit analytical framework for the inverse design of magneto-elastica based on the integral form of the moment equilibrium equations. For convenience of man-ufacture, we consider the strip tapering required to generate a desired 3D shape under magnetic actuation. This formulation directly yields constraints on admissible curvature and rotation fields, enabling a systematic characterization of the feasible design space. By introducing appropriate dimensionless groups, we identify the key parameters governing the competition between magnetic torques and elastic restoring moments. Closed-form solutions are derived for both clamped–free and clamped–clamped configurations, including analytical expressions for the boundary reactions in the latter case. The proposed framework naturally recovers the classical inverse elastica in the absence of magnetic actuation (Liu et al., 2020), ensuring consistency with existing theories. To fabricate a three-dimensional surface with rotational symmetry, an additional constraint on the beam width distribution must be satisfied (Liu et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). Therefore, a tessellation strategy based on stiffness tailoring is finally introduced for the design of discretized morphing surfaces. The theoretical predictions are validated through comparisons with discrete elastic rod simulations and experiments for representative examples. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 formulates the integral equilib-rium equations and develops the inverse-design framework. The general theoretical framework is presented in Sections 2.1 and 2.2. Analytical solutions under different boundary conditions are given in Sections 2.3 and 2.4, followed by a discussion of the connection to the classical elastica in Section 2.5 and the tessellation strategy in Section 2.6. Experimental and numerical validations are presented in Section 3, and conclusions are drawn in Section 4. 

2. Analytical inverse design framework 

In this section, we develop an analytical framework for the inverse design of a magneto-elastica subject to a uniform magnetic field. The formulation is based on the integral form of the moment equilibrium equations, which provides a global description of the internal force–moment balance. The governing relations are then cast in dimensionless form to identify the key parameters control-ling the competition between magnetic and elastic effects. The framework is subsequently applied to inverse design (i.e. determining the tapering that yields a desired 3D shape) under two canonical boundary conditions (BCs): clamped–free and clamped–clamped configurations. Finally, an alternative design strategy based on tessellation is introduced, enabling the extension of the framework to discretized morphing structures. 

2.1. Integral equations of moment equilibrium 

We consider a slender elastic beam of length L with a non-uniform cross-section (Fig. 1(a)), clamped at one or both ends and subject to a uniform magnetic field of magnitude B and orien-tation φ. The beam is assumed to be uniformly magnetized along the tangential direction with magnetization M , so that magnetic actuation induces a distributed body torque along the center-line. The mechanical response is governed by the balance between elastic bending moments and magnetically induced torques. Owing to its slenderness, the beam’s deformation is dominated by bending, and the beam is taken to be inextensible. The beam’s configuration is then described by the tangent angle θ(s), where s ∈ [0 , L ] is the arc-length coordinate measured from the clamped end ( s = 0 ). 2.6. Tessellation 

For an axially symmetric surface, an alternative design principle is to achieve a surface that tesselates upon actuation. As we shall see, this constrains the width profile of the sheet, and so it is not possible to construct a desired surface that also tesselates without another variable, such as the thickness, being varied (Liu et al., 2020). To provide this additional flexibility, we adopt a method to tailor the effective bending stiffness of the magneto-elastica by introducing holes along the centerline (Zhang et al., 2022). Under this framework, tessellation with a desired 3D shape can be realized by prescribing an appropriate effective width distribution while simultaneously ensuring conformal fitting of each individual petal. 3. Illustrative examples and discussion

4. Conclusion 

In this work, we developed an analytical framework for the inverse design of magnetically actuated elastica. Unlike previous approaches that directly invert the differential form of the elastica equations (Lum et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2020), the present formulation is based on the integral form of the moment equilibrium, leading to explicit expressions for the required width distribution and associated design constraints. 14 For clamped–free boundary conditions, the framework enables the systematic characterization of admissible target shapes. For clamped–clamped configurations, the additional degrees of freedom associated with boundary reactions allow for a broader range of realizable geometries. In the absence of magnetic actuation, the proposed formulation naturally reduces to the classical inverse elastica theory (Liu et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2020), ensuring consistency with existing results. The predictive capability of the framework is demonstrated through representative examples, in-cluding semi-circular and cardioid shapes, with excellent agreement between theoretical predictions, physical experiments, and DER simulations. A scaling analysis further reveals that the curvature error scales linearly with deviations in the magnetic actuation parameter, providing a quantita-tive measure of robustness. Finally, we extend the framework to tessellated structures, showing that discretized morphing surfaces with prescribed symmetry can be designed through appropriate stiffness and porosity distributions. Overall, this work establishes a physically interpretable and analytically tractable framework for the inverse design of shape-morphing magneto-elastica, offering new opportunities for programmable soft magnetic actuators, deployable structures, and architected functional materials.

Appendix A. Experiment settings: Fabrication of magneto-elastica 

The fabrication of the magneto-elastica follows a procedure similar to that reported in our recent works ((Zhang et al., 2025)) and is briefly summarized here. A thermoplastic elastomer (SEBS) was dissolved in toluene and mixed with hard ferromagnetic particles (average diameter 5μm) at a weight ratio of 1:2. The Young’s modulus of the resulting composite was determined from uniaxial tensile tests to be E = 4 .5 MPa . The sample thickness was measured to be 0.4 mm . A laser cutter was used to define the desired sample width or to introduce porous structures. Pre-magnetization along the longitudinal direction was performed under a strong magnetic field ( ∼ 4 T), resulting in a remnant magnetization M = 150 kA /m, evaluated as a volume average. Deformation of the magneto-elastica was actuated using a custom-built magnetic field setup, with the field strength monitored by a Gaussmeter.
