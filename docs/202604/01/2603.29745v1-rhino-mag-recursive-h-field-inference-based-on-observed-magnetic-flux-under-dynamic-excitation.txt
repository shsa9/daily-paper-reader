# RHINO-MAG: Recursive H-Field Inference based on Observed Magnetic Flux under Dynamic Excitation 

Hendrik Vater , Oliver Schweins , Lukas H¨ olsch , Wilhelm Kirchg¨ assner ,Till Piepenbrock , and Oliver Wallscheid , Senior Member, IEEE 

Abstract —Driven by the MagNet Challenge 2025 (MC2), in-creased research interest is directed towards modeling transient magnetic fields within ferrite material. An accurate time-resolved and temperature-aware H-field prediction is essential for optimiz-ing magnetic components in applications with quasi-stationary / non-stationary excitation waveforms. Within the scope of this investigation, a selection of model structures with varying degrees of physically motivated structure are compared. Based on a Pareto investigation, a rather black-box gated recurrent unit (GRU) model structure with a graceful initialization setup is found to offer the most attractive model size vs. model accuracy trade-off, while the physics-inspired models performed worse. For a GRU-based model with only 325 parameters, a sequence relative error of 8.02 % and a normalized energy relative error of 1.07 % averaged across five different materials are achieved on unseen test data. With this excellent parameter efficiency, the proposed model won the first place in the performance category of the MC2. 

Index Terms —power magnetics, machine learning, dynamic system modeling 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Magnetics are usually one of the bulkier parts of modern electronics and are responsible for considerable losses [1]. The continuously growing demand toward efficiency, minia-turization, and sustainability places increasing emphasis on optimized designs for magnetic components. The optimization, in turn, requires accurate modeling and understanding of the electromagnetic behavior and corresponding core losses of the magnetic material. The modeling is difficult, because the waveforms resulting from different inputs exhibit varying degrees of hysteresis and saturation that are dependent on the type of material, the core temperature, and the shape, amplitude and frequency of the input waveform [1]–[4]. This, paired with the lack of a satisfactory first-principle model for the underlying processes, has led commonly utilized models to be only accurate approximations for specific operation conditions [4]. While physical first-principle models for the micromagnetic material behavior exist, a transfer of the mi-croscopic physical framework to macroscopic magnetic effects

A. Related work 

For steady-state operation (e.g., in a DC-DC converter), the usual process to estimate core losses is to employ specific approximation equations, e.g., in the form of the Steinmetz equation [5], [6], its various improvements [7]–[11], and more recent developments such as the Stenglein method [3]. While these steady-state core loss models are valid ap-proximations and very effective tools under the appropriate conditions, it has been shown in the context of the MagNet Challenge 2023 (MC1), that they can reach their limits when faced with extensive and varied real-world data [12], [13]. In MC1, this was addressed by encouraging the development of data-driven material models that predict power losses based on waveform, frequency, and temperature in ferrites in steady-state operation [13]. Much progress towards general and accurate models for core loss prediction in steady-state has been made utilizing the tools from machine learning (ML) in the context of the challenge (see, e.g., [4], [12]–[15]). Beyond steady-state operation, there is a vast class of appli-cations in which quasi-stationary or semi-arbitrary excitation of the material is performed due to switched excitation wave-forms, e.g., power factor correction (PFC) circuits, electric mo-tor drives, and power amplifiers [16]. There are simple models based on the Steinmetz equation utilizing the superposition of the major and minor loop core losses for prediction of the core losses in quasi-stationary operation of PFC circuits [1], [17]. However, these concepts cannot easily be extended to semi-arbitrary excitation conditions where the frequencies for major and minor loops constantly change during operation. For instance, for the electric drive, the rotation frequency may change during operation, making the superposition inaccurate. Additionally, superposition inherently assumes linearity which is a simplifying assumption for the nonlinear material behav-ior. Time-resolved modeling allows for more accurate consid-eration of the nonlinearity. Furthermore, it allows modeling of transients, and fully enables circuit simulation in the time domain [4]. Classical approaches for predicting time-resolved hysteretic material behavior include the Preisach [18] and Jiles-Atherton (JA) [19] models, both of which are in essence phenomeno-logical approximations, i.e., models that aim to mathematically describe observed data trajectories and are inspired by physical 

> arXiv:2603.29745v1 [eess.SY] 31 Mar 2026 2

ideas but are not actually faithful to a complete physical framework [20]. In practice, the JA model has been used, e.g., for the esti-mation of core losses in electric machines with non-sinusoidal excitations [21], [22] and for modeling of the material behavior in finite element method (FEM) simulations [23]–[25]. Some issues have been identified with the initial formulation of the model. There is a risk of a non-physical negative dif-ferential permeability [26] and, furthermore, non-closure of minor loops and consequential drifting of the estimation is a known problem when the excitation is not a closed loop [20]. Multiple extensions of the JA model have been proposed that aim to improve the prediction quality including more precise prediction of minor loops [27]–[30]. However, most of the existing applications of the JA model are limited in the scope of operating conditions. Some first approaches with adaptable parameters have been proposed in order to allow more general application (see, e.g., [26], [31]). The Preisach model has been used for iron loss estimations with different excitation waveforms (e.g., [32]–[34]) and also has been compared to the JA model in prediction performance for DC-biased hysteresis behavior [34]. The Preisach model was found to be more accurate especially for increasing DC-bias, but also required more computational effort [34]. In [35], it was found that the proposed inverse Preisach was more accurate and required less computational effort. However, they utilized a closed form for the underlying Everett function, reducing the flexibility of the Preisach model. Ferrite materials are made up of an arbitrarily oriented and shaped grain structure on the micrometer scale that heavily dominates the macroscopic magnetic behavior. Physical mod-els based on micromagnetic particle interactions such as the Landau-Lifshitz-Gilbert equation (LLG) [36], [37] operate at the nanometer scale. Transferring those to real-world mag-netic material probes with heterogeneous, defective structures remains an open question. In summary, a first-principle model to explain magnetic hysteresis that is reasonably applicable on a macroscopic scale is missing [4]. Existing phenomenological, data-driven models are only employed to different, small subproblems and never really thoroughly compared on various operating conditions. This makes it difficult to determine which model types are most effective for varied and semi-arbitrary excitations. 

B. MagNet Challenge 2025 

Driven by the gap in physical understanding, modeling ca-pabilities, and heterogeneous data for time-resolved magnetic material behavior and further motivated by the success of data-driven models for steady-state operation in MC1, MagNet Challenge 2025 (MC2) [16] emphasizes transient magnetic material behavior. The goal is to reconstruct and understand the time-resolved trajectories of the magnetic field H, given histories of magnetic flux density B and other relevant op-eration information to enable a better understanding of the physical behavior and more accurate predictions of core losses. For this purpose, a corresponding data set 1 is provided by the competition hosts [16]. First data-driven models have been proposed by the competition hosts for such time-resolved, tran-sient modeling of magnetic behavior in [38], [39] utilizing long short-term memory (LSTM)-based recurrent neural networks (RNNs). C. Contribution 

Considering the state of the art for modeling of magnetic material behavior with transient excitation and the context of MC2, the following contributions are made: 1) Design of a parameter-efficient gated recurrent unit (GRU) model archetype for time-resolved modeling of magnetic material behavior with an intuitive and effective concept for warmup of the model’s hidden state. 2) Extensive comparison of a selection of model archetypes for a variety of model sizes with respect to their pre-diction accuracy in order to provide a Pareto front and, therefore, a basis of comparison for further magnetic transient modeling approaches. 3) Open-source release of the RHINO-MAG modeling framework containing all of the presented modeling ap-proaches online. 2 The implementation is made in JAX 3

Python library for seamless utilization of accelerator hardware (GPUs, TPUs) and just-in-time (JIT) compi-lation. With only 325 parameters and an average SRE score of

E. LLG-based modeling 

The idea of this approach is once more to utilize physics-informed machine learning (cf. Sec. III-C4) to incorporate a physical equation with the hidden state of the RNNs. This could provide a physical interpretation for the hidden state, which could yield insights into the internal structure of the material, and give the RNN a guideline on how the hidden state should propagate. 1) LLG PDE simulation: As a preparation step, it is tested how well a simulation of the LLG equation may approximate macroscopic hysteresis effects by summing over the micro-scopic magnetization. For this, the Python micromagnetic simulation NeuralMag 7 [47] was utilized. The main issue is that not only the spatial scale is not properly fitting, but also the time scale. The considered data was measured with τ = 62 .5 ns , while such micromagnetic simulations are generally performed at τ ≈ 10 ps or with even smaller time steps (Fig. 10 in [48]), which would require roughly 6000 steps per data point from the MC2 data base. IV. R ESULTS 

The proposed models are evaluated on a subset of 5 from the 15 materials provided in the MagNetX database, each of which holds a specific challenge for modeling. An overview over the 5 testing materials is provided in Tab. III. After the conclusion of MC2 a training data set D(m2)train and a testing data set D(m2)test are available for each of the materials. 

A. Model selection for submission to MC2 

The final model selection for the submission to MC2 was done before D(m2)test was available in order to have a true training-test split. Therefore, the following considerations are done purely on D(m2)train for each of the materials. From previous tests, the decision was already made to utilize the GRU-P model. An important aspect of this model is the size, which is defined by the number of hidden units and the number of inputs. In the following, the model size will be changed by varying the number of hidden units in the RNNs. The tradeoff between the model size measured by the number of parameters and the model accuracy is to be investigated. The model accuracies are measured by the SRE (5) and NERE (6) score on unseen test data. For the following test, for each of the materials D(m2)train is split into a separate training D(m2)train ,train ,evaluation D(m2)train ,eval , and test set D(m2)train ,test .Overall, the choice for the submitted models is made for a good tradeoff between model size and accuracy motivated by the preliminary Pareto investigation depicted in Fig. 6. However, it is not claimed that this is the best choice for the general case. For instance, if the chosen model application permits a higher computation budget and memory footprint or if there are specific accuracy constraints to the performance of the model, a larger or even smaller model might be better suited. Hence, the good model size vs. accuracy scalability of the GRU-based approach through the choice of cell count is one of its strengths and allows for easy adaptation depending on application requirements. Tab. IV summarizes key performance indicators of the 8 cell GRU models that were submitted as final results for the MC2. The provided model size is measured on disk (without any pruning or quantization). In Fig. 7 and Fig. 8, the prediction performance of the submitted model architecture is shown exemplary for a full sequence from unseen data.

V. C ONCLUSION 

A variety of modeling structures for the prediction of time-resolved magnetic material behavior were developed, imple-mented, and compared in the scope of this work. It was found that an almost purely data-driven model utilizing a single GRU layer could provide excellent parameter efficiency in terms of model size to model accuracy for small model sizes. As further incorporation of physically motivated ideas could not improve the performance of this model, it remains an open question how to properly inject physically motivated structure into the model and what kind of physical equations are actually helpful for the macroscopic problem at hand. To complement the presented work in the future, interesting directions might be to perform a hyperparameter optimization for the proposed model structures in order to ensure that they perform as best as possible. Furthermore, the presented Pareto investigations should be extended with the other models that were submitted for MC2 to enable proper comparison throughout different parameterizations. Additionally, it would be interesting to consider transfer learning for the provided model structures such that the model can be simply adapted to a new material with only limited measurement data (cf.
