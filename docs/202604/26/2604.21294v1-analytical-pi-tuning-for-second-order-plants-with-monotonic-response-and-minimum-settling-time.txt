# Abstract 

Background : Tuning proportional -integral (PI) controllers for second -order plants to achieve 

monotonic step response with minimum settling time is an important problem in analytical 

control design. Existing methods address these objectives only partially or require numerical 

optimization. 

Methods : A closed -form analytical solution is derived through pole placement in the 

framework of Astrom and Hagglund . The key insight is that designing the closed -loop poles 

slower than the fast plant pole forces pole -zero cancellation of the slow plant pole as a 

consequence, not an assumption. The critically damped condition is then applied to minimize 

settling time. 

Results : The optimal PI parameters are K=T1/( 4KpT2 ), Ti=T1 , where T1 and T2 are the plant 

time constants and Kp is the plant gain. No free parameter remains. The resulting closed -loop 

system possesses universal robustness properties independent of plant parameters: 

maximum complementary sensitivity Mt = 1, maximum sensitivity Ms = 1.155, and phase 

margin PM = 76.35 degree .

Conclusions : The proposed tuning formulas are explicit, analytically proven, and apply 

directly to any stable second -order plant with two real poles. Simulation results across six plant 

configurations confirm the analytical predictions exactly. The notation follows Astrom and 

Hagglund [5] throughout. 

Keywords: PI controller; second -order plant; pole placement; critically damped; monotonic 

response; settling time; robustness 

# 1. Introduction 

The proportional -integral (PI) controller remains the most widely used feedback controller in 

industrial process control due to its simplicity and effectiveness. Tuning PI controllers to 

achieve satisfactory transient response has been an active research a rea for decades. The 

first systematic tuning method was proposed by Ziegler and Nichols [1], which has since been 

refined by numerous authors [2,3]. 

The plant model considered in this work describes a broad class of physical systems 

encountered in practice. Mass -spring -damper mechanical systems, series RLC electrical 

circuits, and armature -controlled DC motors with negligible armature inductance all yi eld 

transfer functions of this form with two real negative poles [4]. Many higher -order systems are 

also routinely approximated as second -order models to facilitate analysis and controller design 

[4]. The proposed tuning method therefore applies directly t o a wide range of engineering 

applications without requiring model reduction or special treatment. 

A common requirement in many industrial applications, including precision positioning, 

thermal control, and dosing systems, is that the step response must be non -overshooting and 

monotonically non -decreasing. Overshoot can cause damage, quality degradation , or safety 

violations. Settling time must simultaneously be minimized to achieve high throughput. These 

two objectives, zero overshoot and minimum settling time, are competing in general, and no 

analytical solution has been reported for PI control of seco nd -order plants. Astrom and Hagglund show in Example 6.7 of [5] that PI control can achieve any prescribed 

pole configuration (α, ζ), with the scale factor  𝜔 0 determined by the process dynamics. 

However, the optimal selection of α and ζ to achieve monotonic response with minimum 

settling time was not addressed. 

Nguyen and Nguyen [6] proposed a PID controller for first -order and second -order systems 

that guarantees zero overshoot and arbitrary settling time. For second -order plants, their 

method employs a PID controller with both plant poles cancelled, reducing th e closed -loop to 

first order. While effective, the method requires derivative action and does not provide a 

minimum settling time criterion, as the proportional gain remains a free parameter determined 

by the desired settling time specification. 

Albatran et al. [7] proposed explicit PI and PID tuning formulas for underdamped second -order 

systems based on particle swarm optimization and cascaded curve fitting. Near -optimal gains 

are obtained as functions of the plant damping ratio and natural frequ ency, with a maximum 

overshoot below five percent for PI control. While the formulas are explicit, they are 

numerically derived rather than analytically proven, and strict monotonicity of the step 

response is not guaranteed. 

The present work answers the open question of Example 6.7 in [5]. For a second -order plant 

controlled by a PI controller, the optimal α and ζ are derived analytically. The key insight is 

that designing the closed -loop poles slower than the fast plant pole — exactly the condition 

identified by Astrom and Hagglund for the first -order case — forces pole -zero cancellation of 

the slow plant pole as a consequence, not an assumption. Combined with the critically damped 

condition ζ = 1 for minimum settling time, this uniquely determines all controller parameters in 

close d form with no free parameter remaining. It is further shown that the resulting solution 

possesses universal robustness properties independent of plant parameters.

# 6. Conclusion 

A closed -form analytical PI tuning method is presented that guarantees monotonic step 

response with minimum settling time for second -order plants . The optimal controller 

parameters K=T 1/(4K pT2) and T i=T 1 are determined solely by the plant time constants T 1, T 2

and plant gain K p.

A further result is established: the proposed tuning yields universal robustness properties 

independent of the plant parameters K p, T 1 and T 2. The maximum complementary sensitivity 

is Mt = 1, the maximum sensitivity is Ms = 2/√3 ≈ 1.155, and the phase margin is PM = 76.35°.
