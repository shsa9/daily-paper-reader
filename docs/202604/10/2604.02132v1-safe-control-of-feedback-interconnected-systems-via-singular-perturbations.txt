# Safe Control of Feedback-Interconnected Systems via Singular Perturbations 

Stefano Di Gregorio, Guido Carnevale, Giuseppe Notarstefano 

Abstract 

Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) have emerged as a powerful tool in the design of safety-critical controllers for nonlinear systems. In modern applications, complex systems often involve the feedback interconnection of subsystems evolving at different timescales, e.g., two parts from different physical domains (e.g., the electrical and mechanical parts of robotic systems) or a physical plant and an (optimization or control) algorithm. In these scenarios, safety constraints often involve only a portion of the overall system. Inspired by singular perturbations for stability analysis, we develop a formal procedure to lift a safety certificate designed on a reduced-order model to the overall feedback-interconnected system. Specifically, we show that under a sufficient timescale separation between slow and fast dynamics, a composite CBF can be designed to certify the forward invariance of the safe set for the interconnected system. As a result, the online safety filter only needs to be solved for the lower-dimensional, reduced-order model. We numerically test the proposed approach on: (i) a robotic arm with joint motor dynamics, and (ii) a physical plant driven by an optimization algorithm. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Autonomous and complex systems operating in real world scenarios require the design of control algorithms capable of enforcing strict safety operational constraints. This challenging requirement has given rise to intense research efforts in the control literature, as reviewed in the recent survey [1]. Among existing methods, Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) have emerged as a powerful tool for designing safety-critical controllers for nonlinear systems (see the comprehensive references [2]–[4]). Their versatility has been demonstrated across different domains, including robotics [5], autonomous vehicles [6], and human-robot interaction [7]. A major structural limitation arises when the control input does not directly affect the safety constraint, leading to a high relative degree. To address this, High-Order CBFs (HOCBFs) [8] have been proposed to propagate safety via successive differentiations of the constraint. Despite its effectiveness, this approach requires a uniform relative degree across the safe set. This limitation has motivated a shift toward more flexible architectures, such as leveraging input-output feedback linearization (see, e.g., [9]), rectified CBFs [10], or designs based on the backstepping technique [11]. Parallel to these developments, the integration of safety with other objectives has been widely explored. The combination of Control Lyapunov Functions (CLFs) and CBFs, allows for the simultaneous pursuit of safety and stability objectives. To this end, authors in [12] unify them under a single optimization-based framework. This unification has been further generalized by the development of a universal formula for safe stabilization [13], providing an analytical construction for the control law. To handle practical uncertainties, these formulations have been extended to enforce safety and stability under input disturbances using ISSf-CBFs (input-to-state safe CBFs, [14]). The conflict between reaching a target state and maintaining safety has been addressed in [15], where a hybrid CLF-CBF formulation is proposed to overcome intrinsic topological deadlocks. Recently, an extension to model uncertainties [16] is derived via an adaptive approach obtained by combining of a CBF, a CLF and a parameter error term. Beyond structural control design, a second major research direction focuses on the direct integration of CBFs into online dynamic optimization algorithms. In this framework, safety-critical control is reformulated as a constrained nonlinear program, where gradient flows are augmented with CBF-based inputs to ensure forward invariance of the feasible set [17]. This approach has been further extended to the context of feedback optimization (see, e.g., [18]–[20]). Specifically, authors of [21] propose a safe feedback optimization method that enforces state constraints at all times. However, the increasing complexity of modern control architectures leads to nontrivial and/or unknown interconnected dynamics, for which designing CBFs is challenging or even impossible. To mitigate this issue, there is a growing effort to develop approaches that synthesize CBFs for reduced-order models (see the recent survey [22]). Several of these approaches rely on specific system structures. For instance, when the system is in strict-feedback form, safety can be propagated from a virtual controller to the actual input via backstepping [23]. Alternative strategies include energy-based augmentations [24] or the model-free tracking of safe reference velocities [25]. Closer to our proposed methodology, the work [26] addresses safety in multi-agent systems by resorting to the system structure typically used in singular perturbations. Despite these advancements, a systematic framework to guarantee the safety of generally interconnected systems, especially when dealing with reduced-order models embedded within complex feedback loops, remains an open challenge. The main contribution of this paper is the establishment of a formal framework, based on singular perturbation (or timescale separation) theory, to guarantee the safety of feedback-interconnected systems. By adapting techniques from singular perturbation analysis, we show that if the neglected subsystem evolves on a sufficiently faster timescale than the reduced-order model, then a composite CBF can be constructed to certify the forward invariance of the safe set for the full interconnected system. Notably, our approach applies to broad classes of nonlinear dynamics without requiring a strict cascade structure or restrictive inversion-based feedback laws. A. Reduced-Order Safety Certificates 

We are interested in designing safety certificates on reduced-order models and then lift them to the overall feedback-interconnected system. Purposely, we rely on the following three requirements. First, the safety specifications are defined solely in terms of the slow subsystem state x in (2a). More in detail, we introduce Sx ⊆ Rn as the safe set for the slow subsystem (2a) and, accordingly, the safe set for the overall system (2) is defined as 

S := {(x, z ) ∈ Rn × Rp | x ∈ S x}. (3) Second, the fast subsystem (2b) admits an equilibrium function parametrized in x and u.

Assumption 2: There exists a continuously differentiable function zeq : Sx × U → Rp such that 

0 = g(zeq (x, u ), x, u ).

for all x ∈ S x and u ∈ U . □

Third, we assume the availability of a robustly-safe controller for a reduced-order model of (2a). Formally, we consider system (2) on the manifold {(x, z, u ) ∈ S x × Rp × U | z = zeq (x, u )}. Namely, we introduce the reduced dynamics

∇h(x)⊤frs (x, π (x)) ≥ − α(h(x)) + η, (5) for all x ∈ C . □

The goal of our paper is to establish sufficient conditions to lift the safety certificate in Assumption 3 thus guaranteeing safety of the overall system (2).

In this section, we provide a safety certificate for the interconnected system (2) based on the reduced-order safety h and the stability properties of the boundary layer system (6) (cf. Assumption 4), respectively. To this end, we introduce the composite CBF function V : C × Rp → R defined as 

V (x, z ) := h(x) − U (z − zeq (x, π (x))) , (15) and we denote as CV := {(x, z ) ∈ Rn × Rp | V (x, z ) ≥ 0} its zero-superlevel set. 

Theorem 1: Consider system (2) and let Assumptions 1, 2, 3, and 4 hold. Then, there exists ¯ϵ > 0 such that, for all 

ϵ ∈ (0 , ¯ϵ), the set CV is forward invariant for the closed-loop system 

˙x(t) = f (x(t), z (t), π (x(t))) 

ϵ ˙z(t) = g(z(t), x (t), π (x(t))) . □

The proof of Theorem 1 is provided in Appendix A. To illustrate the theoretical guarantees provided by Theorem 1, we consider as a toy example the following two-dimensional singularly perturbed system 

˙x(t) = z(t) − x(t), (17a)

Sx = {x ∈ R | β2 − x2 ≥ 0},

for some β > 0, and the CBF h : R → R defined as 

h(x) := β2 − x2.

Namely, in this case, C ≡ S x. To achieve this safety goal while tracking a nominal policy πdes (x) = 2 sin( x), the actual control input u(t) = π(x) is computed via a CBF-QP based on the reduced-order dynamics. To satisfy Assumption 3, this QP strictly enforces the safety margin η > 0, guaranteeing that ∇h(x)⊤frs (x, π (x)) ≥ − α(h(x)) + η. This construction introduces a restriction on the admissible slow states. Indeed, (x, z ) ∈ C V ⇐⇒ h(x) ≥ U (z − zeq (x, π (x))) and, thus, h(x) must be sufficiently large to compensate for the energy stored in the fast state z when it deviates from the equilibrium zeq (x, π (x)) . zeq (x, π (x)) (dashed blue curve). These boundaries coincide with the “ideal” reduced ones only when the fast state exactly lies in the equilibrium manifold (i.e., when z = zeq (x, π (x)) ). 

b) Timescale Separation and Safety: Consistently with singular perturbation results for stability, Theorem 1 captures the timescale separation required to guarantee safety. In the ideal case ϵ → 0, the fast state would instantaneously converge to the equilibrium zeq (x, π (x)) , thus allowing the slow system to behave exactly as the ideal reduced one and, thus, to preserve safety by Assumption 3. However, for ϵ̸ = 0 , the timescale separation is not perfect. Here, the strict safety margin η plays a fundamental role, acting as a designed robustness buffer against the perturbation terms due to z(t)̸ = zeq (x(t), π (x(t))) . As shown in the time-domain plot of x(t) in Fig. 3b, when ϵ is below the critical threshold ¯ϵ, z(t) converges rapidly enough that the magnitude of the perturbation is absorbed by η, thus preserving safety. In this paper, we developed a singular perturbation framework for safety, which is the first of its kind in the literature. More specifically, we considered feedback interconnections between two subsystems, referred to as the slow and fast subsystems, respectively. We then provided sufficient conditions to guarantee safety of the overall interconnected system, based on reduced-order safety properties of the slow subsystem and stability properties of the fast one. We illustrated the applicability of our theoretical results in two settings. The first is the safe control of a robotic arm with joint motors.
